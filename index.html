1776
Declaration of Independence
Thomas Jefferson
America's break-up letter to Britain. All people have unalienable rights — life, liberty, pursuit of happiness. Government gets its power from the consent of the governed.
Core argument: Social contract theory (Locke). If government fails to protect rights, people may overthrow it.

Exam connections: Compare to Federalist 51 (why government is needed) and MLK's Letter (both invoke natural/moral law).
LUNG: Liberty, Unalienable rights, Natural law, Government by consent
1781
Articles of Confederation
First U.S. Constitution
America's first attempt at national government — and a famous failure. States held almost all power. Congress couldn't tax, enforce laws, or regulate trade.
Key weaknesses: No president. No federal courts. One vote per state. 9/13 states to pass laws. Unanimous to amend.

Why it matters: Its failures directly explain every feature of the Constitution written to replace it.
CAN'T: Congress Can't tax, Armed forces voluntary, No executive, Trade chaos
1787
The U.S. Constitution
Including Bill of Rights & All Amendments
Supreme law of the land. Establishes 3 branches, separates powers, creates federalism, and protects rights through amendments.
Key concepts: Separation of powers, checks & balances, federalism, popular sovereignty, limited government.

Structure: Preamble → 7 Articles → 27 Amendments. Art. I = Congress, Art. II = President, Art. III = Courts.
SPELL: Separation of powers, Popular sovereignty, Elected reps, Limited gov, Law is supreme
Federalist
Federalist No. 10
James Madison
A large republic is better at controlling factions (interest groups) than a small one. More people = more diversity = no single faction dominates.
The argument: Factions are dangerous but unavoidable. Don't remove liberty — control factions' effects. A big republic dilutes their power.

Exam connection: Direct counter to Brutus 1, which says big republic = unresponsive government.
10-FAN: Fed. 10 → Factions Avoided in a big Nation
Anti-Federalist
Brutus No. 1
Anonymous (Robert Yates)
Argues against the Constitution. Large republic = government too far from the people. Necessary & Proper + Supremacy Clause would let Congress swallow state power.
Key fears: Standing army could oppress. Federal power would crush states. Representatives can't truly represent diverse populations.

Remember: Brutus = Anti-Federalist = wants small government, strong states.
Brutus STABS big gov: States lose power, Taxation without care, Army dangers, Big gov = tyranny, Small republic is better
Federalist
Federalist No. 51
James Madison
"Ambition must be made to counteract ambition." Each branch needs tools to defend itself from the others. Checks & balances protect liberty because men aren't angels.
Key idea: If men were angels, no government necessary. Since they're not, we need both government AND checks on government. Federalism = double security.
51 = CHECKS: Fed. 51 → Checks & balances are How we Ensure each branch Controls others, Keeping Safeguards in place
Federalist
Federalist No. 70
Alexander Hamilton
Argues for a single, energetic president. One person = clear accountability + quick decisions. Multiple executives would cause conflict and weakness.
Key argument: "Energy in the executive is a leading character in the definition of good government." A single president can act fast and be held responsible.
70 = ONE strong E: Hamilton says ONE energetic executive is best
Federalist
Federalist No. 78
Alexander Hamilton
Courts are the "least dangerous branch." Defends lifetime judicial tenure and anticipates judicial review — courts can strike down unconstitutional laws.
Key argument: Judges need lifetime tenure ("good behavior") to be free from political pressure. Courts protect minority rights against temporary majorities.

Exam connection: Directly foreshadows Marbury v. Madison.
78 = JUDGE for LIFE: Judges Under Duty Guard Everyone, serving for LIFE
1963
Letter from Birmingham Jail
Martin Luther King Jr.
MLK defends nonviolent protest against unjust laws. An unjust law degrades human dignity. Citizens have a moral responsibility to disobey unjust laws openly.
Key distinction: Just law = aligns with moral/natural law. Unjust law = conflicts with it. Civil disobedience must be open, loving, and accept punishment.

Exam connections: Declaration of Independence (natural rights), 1st Amendment (protest), Brown v. Board (civil rights).
MLK's JUST rule: Just laws uplift, Unjust laws degrade, Stand nonviolently, Take the punishment
⌕
Judicial Review
Marbury v. Madison · 1803
The Supreme Court can strike down laws that violate the Constitution. Established judicial review — the foundation of court power.
Simple story: Adams appointed Marbury as a judge last minute. Jefferson refused to deliver the commission. Marshall said: even though Marbury deserves it, the law giving the Court this specific power was unconstitutional — so we won't enforce it, but we ARE the ones who say what's constitutional.
Marbury = Map: Just like a map tells you what's there, the Court tells you what the law is.
Implied Powers & Federal Supremacy
McCulloch v. Maryland · 1819
Congress has implied powers (Necessary & Proper Clause). States cannot tax the federal government. Federal law is supreme.
Simple story: Maryland tried to tax the national bank. Marshall said: (1) Congress can create a bank even without explicit constitutional authority. (2) "The power to tax involves the power to destroy" — states can't destroy federal institutions.
McCulloch = Must Comply: States must comply with federal supremacy.
Free Speech Limits
Schenck v. United States · 1919
Free speech can be limited if it creates a "clear and present danger." Distributing anti-draft leaflets during WWI wasn't protected.
Key concept: Speech isn't absolute. "Shouting fire in a crowded theater." Context matters — wartime raises the stakes. Later modified by Brandenburg (danger must be imminent).
Schenck = "Shhh!" Sometimes you have to be quiet (speech has limits).
Equal Protection (14th Amendment)
Brown v. Board of Education · 1954
Racial segregation in public schools is unconstitutional. "Separate but equal" is inherently unequal. Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896).
Why it matters: Overturned 58 years of Plessy. Unanimous Warren Court decision. Used Equal Protection Clause, not Due Process. Led to desegregation orders nationwide.
Brown BUSTED the "separate but equal" myth.
One Person, One Vote
Baker v. Carr · 1962
Federal courts CAN hear cases about legislative apportionment. Paved the way for "one person, one vote."
Simple story: Tennessee hadn't redrawn districts in 60 years, so rural voters had way more power per person than city voters. Court said this is a justiciable (court-worthy) issue — not a political question.
Baker BAKES equal slices — every vote should be the same size.
Establishment Clause
Engel v. Vitale · 1962
Government-sponsored prayer in public schools violates the Establishment Clause, even if the prayer is voluntary and non-denominational.
Simple story: New York schools had a short, voluntary prayer. Supreme Court said it doesn't matter — government writing and sponsoring ANY prayer = establishment of religion.
Engel = End the prayer: Government can't write school prayers.
Right to Counsel (6th Amendment)
Gideon v. Wainwright · 1963
Every criminal defendant has the right to an attorney. If they can't afford one, the state must provide one.
Simple story: Gideon was accused of breaking into a pool hall, was too poor to hire a lawyer, represented himself, and lost. Unanimous Supreme Court said: 6th Amendment applies to states via 14th Amendment.
Gideon Gets a lawyer — even if broke, you get one.
Symbolic Speech (1st Amendment)
Tinker v. Des Moines · 1969
Students don't "shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate." Wearing black armbands to protest Vietnam = protected symbolic speech.
Key limit: Schools CAN restrict speech that causes "substantial disruption." Armbands were silent and passive — not disruptive. Know the substantial disruption test.
Tinker = Think (free thought & expression in school).
Freedom of Press / Prior Restraint
New York Times Co. v. United States · 1971
Government cannot use prior restraint (censorship before publication) to stop newspapers from printing the Pentagon Papers.
Simple story: NYT got leaked secret Vietnam War documents. Nixon tried to stop publication. Court said: government faces a very heavy burden to justify prior restraint — and didn't meet it here.
NYT = No Yielding to censorship.
Free Exercise Clause
Wisconsin v. Yoder · 1972
Amish families cannot be forced to send children to school past 8th grade. Free Exercise Clause protects sincere religious practice.
Key balance: State's interest in education vs. family's religious freedom. Court sided with religion — the Amish community had a deeply sincere, centuries-old practice that state schooling would destroy.
Yoder = Your religion, Your rules (mostly).
Right to Privacy (Due Process)
Roe v. Wade · 1973 · Overturned 2022
Constitution's implied right to privacy protects abortion rights. Overturned by Dobbs v. Jackson (2022) — still on AP Gov required list.
Exam note: Even though Roe was overturned, know the original holding AND that it was overturned by Dobbs. Know the key concept: "penumbras" of the Bill of Rights create an implied right to privacy (from Griswold v. Connecticut).
Roe = Right of privacy (for now).
Redistricting & Equal Protection
Shaw v. Reno · 1993
Race cannot be the predominant factor in drawing congressional districts. Racial gerrymandering violates Equal Protection even with good intentions.
Simple story: NC drew an oddly-shaped district to create a majority-Black district. Court said drawing lines purely by race = constitutionally suspect, even if motivated by representation goals.
Shaw = Shape matters (can't use race to draw weird shapes).
Commerce Clause / Federalism
United States v. Lopez · 1995
Congress cannot use the Commerce Clause to ban guns in school zones. That's not economic activity — it's a local matter. Landmark federalism case.
Why it matters: For 60+ years, Congress used Commerce Clause to regulate nearly everything. Lopez said: there are limits. Federal power can't reach purely local, non-economic activity.
Lopez = Limits on Congress.
2nd Amendment / Incorporation
McDonald v. Chicago · 2010
The 2nd Amendment right to bear arms applies to state and local governments, not just the federal government (via 14th Amendment incorporation).
Background: Heller (2008) said 2nd Amendment protects individual gun ownership federally. McDonald extended that protection to states via incorporation doctrine.
McDonald = Makes 2nd Amendment apply everywhere (like McDonald's is everywhere).
Campaign Finance / Free Speech
Citizens United v. FEC · 2010
Corporations and unions have 1st Amendment rights. Government cannot limit their independent political expenditures. Led to rise of Super PACs.
Key holding: "Money = speech" for political spending. But direct donations to campaigns still regulated. Massive controversy — opened door to unlimited outside spending in elections.
Citizens United = Cash Unlimited.

1
Religion · Assembly · Press · Petition · Speech — the five freedoms. Most tested amendment.
Two religion clauses: Establishment Clause (gov can't promote religion) + Free Exercise Clause (gov can't stop your practice).

Key cases: Engel (Establishment), Yoder (Free Exercise), Tinker (Speech), NYT v. US (Press), Citizens United (Speech).
RAPPS: Religion, Assembly, Press, Petition, Speech

2nd Amendment — Right to Bear Arms
2
Individual right to keep and bear arms (Heller). Applies to states per McDonald v. Chicago.
★ High Yield
4th Amendment — Search & Seizure
4
Police need a warrant based on probable cause to search you or your property. Protects against unreasonable searches and seizures.
★ High Yield
5th Amendment — Due Process & Self-Incrimination
5
You cannot be forced to testify against yourself. Government must follow due process before depriving you of life, liberty, or property. No double jeopardy.
Know It
6th Amendment — Right to Counsel
6
Right to an attorney (Gideon), speedy and public trial, impartial jury, and to know the charges against you.
★ High Yield
10th Amendment — Reserved Powers
10
Powers not given to the federal government are reserved to the states or the people. Foundation of federalism.
Key in US v. Lopez — Court used states' reserved powers to limit federal Commerce Clause reach. Connects to Brutus 1's argument for state sovereignty.
10th = TENS of powers go to States.
Civil Rights Amendments — 13, 14, 15
Civil Rights
13th Amendment — Abolished Slavery
13
Slavery and involuntary servitude are abolished. Exception: punishment for crime. Ratified 1865 after the Civil War.
★ VERY High Yield
14th Amendment — Equal Protection
14
All born in the US are citizens. States must provide equal protection and due process. Used to incorporate Bill of Rights to states.
Three key clauses: Citizenship (born here = citizen), Due Process (states must be fair), Equal Protection (no discrimination).

Used in: Brown v. Board, Gideon, McDonald, Baker v. Carr, Shaw v. Reno — virtually every civil rights & incorporation case.
14th = 14-karat GOLD of amendments — the most used amendment ever.
Civil Rights
15th Amendment — Voting Rights for Black Men
15
Cannot deny the vote based on race, color, or previous servitude. Ratified 1870. Poll taxes and literacy tests still suppressed Black votes for decades after.

17
Before 1913: state legislatures chose senators. After 17th: voters directly elect senators. Made Senate more democratic and responsive.
★ High Yield
19th — Women's Suffrage
19
Cannot deny the vote based on sex. Ratified 1920 after decades of suffragist activism.
★ High Yield
22nd — Presidential Term Limits
22
No president can serve more than 2 terms. Passed after FDR served 4 terms. Ratified 1951.
★ High Yield

24
Cannot require payment to vote in federal elections. Eliminated a major tool used to suppress Black voting in the South. Ratified 1964.

The four FRQ types

1. SCOTUS Comparison FRQ: Compare a required case to a non-required one. Identify similarity in facts, then explain how they ruled differently and why.

2. Argument Essay: Write a structured argument using 2+ required cases and/or foundational documents as evidence.

3. Concept Application: Apply a real-world scenario to a political concept (federalism, separation of powers, civil liberties, etc.).

4. Quantitative Analysis: Interpret a chart, graph, or data table and connect findings to a political concept.

Document connections cheat sheet

↔
Federalist 10 vs. Brutus 1: Both about large republics — Madison says safer, Brutus says dangerous. Classic exam comparison.
→
Federalist 51 + Constitution: Both explain separation of powers and why checks & balances protect liberty.
→
Federalist 78 + Marbury v. Madison: Hamilton predicted judicial review; Marbury established it in practice.
↔
Letter from Birmingham Jail + Declaration of Independence: Both invoke natural/moral law to justify action against unjust authority.
→
Articles of Confederation → Constitution: The Articles' failures directly explain every feature of the Constitution that replaced it.

Federalism in one paragraph

Federalism = power split between national and state governments. Expressed powers = written in Constitution. Implied powers = Necessary & Proper Clause (McCulloch). Reserved powers = 10th Amendment (states). Concurrent powers = shared (taxing, courts). Supremacy Clause = federal law wins conflicts. Key cases: McCulloch (fed power), Lopez (fed limits), McDonald (incorporation).

Civil liberties vs. civil rights

Civil liberties = protections FROM government action (1st, 4th, 5th, 6th Amendment rights).
Civil rights = equal treatment BY government (14th Amendment, Voting Rights Act, Brown v. Board).

Easy memory: Liberties = freedom from. Rights = equal treatment.

Incorporation doctrine

The 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause has been used to "incorporate" (apply) most Bill of Rights protections to state governments — not just the federal government. This is how Gideon (6th), McDonald (2nd), and Engel (1st) all apply to state laws. Not all amendments are incorporated — the 3rd and 7th largely are not.

MCQ strategy

1. Read the stimulus (chart, quote, scenario) carefully first — the answer is almost always in it.
2. Look for key words: "primarily," "most likely," "best explains."
3. For document questions — recall the author and core argument before looking at choices.
4. For court cases — know the constitutional clause, not just the ruling.
5. Eliminate answers that are factually wrong about the document or case, even if they sound reasonable.

Congress — Structure & Composition
House of Representatives
435 total members · Seats based on population (reapportioned every 10 years after census) · Term = 2 years · Must be at least 25 years old, 7 years a citizen · All revenue bills must originate here · Impeaches by simple majority
218 votes = simple majority needed to pass a bill or impeach
290 votes = 2/3 majority needed to override a presidential veto
Speaker of the House = 2nd in line to presidency (after VP)
Largest state delegation: California (currently ~52 seats)
House = H for Hot-tempered & fast: 2-year terms, closest to the people, starts revenue bills.
Senate
100 total members · 2 per state regardless of population · Term = 6 years (staggered — 1/3 up for election every 2 years) · Must be at least 30 years old, 9 years a citizen · Confirms appointments, ratifies treaties
51 votes = simple majority to pass a bill or confirm a nominee
60 votes = cloture (ending a filibuster) — 3/5 of all senators
67 votes = 2/3 to ratify a treaty or convict/remove a president
VP = President of the Senate, votes only to break ties
Filibuster: unlimited debate tactic — only the Senate has it
"Senate = Slow & Serious": 6-year terms, older age req, more deliberate body.
Constitutional Thresholds
Amendment Process — Two Paths
Path 1 (used for all 27 amendments): 2/3 of both House & Senate propose → 3/4 of state legislatures ratify (38 states)

Path 2 (never used): 2/3 of state legislatures call a convention → 3/4 of states ratify
The president has no role in amending the Constitution — no signature required. Congress proposes, states ratify.

Under the Articles of Confederation, amendment required unanimous consent of all 13 states — one reason the Articles failed.
"2/3 propose, 3/4 ratify" — the two fractions you must remember.
Presidential Veto & Override
President has 10 days to sign or veto a bill · 2/3 of both chambers can override a veto · Pocket veto: if Congress adjourns within 10 days and president doesn't sign, bill dies
Override requires 290 votes in House (2/3 of 435) + 67 votes in Senate (2/3 of 100).

Overrides are extremely rare — most vetoes are sustained because it's very hard to get 2/3 of both chambers to agree.
Impeachment Process
Step 1 — House: Simple majority (218+) votes to impeach (formally charge)
Step 2 — Senate: 2/3 majority (67+) votes to convict and remove from office
Chief Justice presides over presidential impeachment trial
Presidents impeached by House: Andrew Johnson (1868), Bill Clinton (1998), Donald Trump (2019, 2021) — none were removed by the Senate.

Richard Nixon resigned before the House voted.
"House charges, Senate judges" — like a grand jury vs. a trial jury.
Electoral College & Elections
Electoral College Breakdown
538 total electoral votes · 270 needed to win · Each state gets electors = House seats + 2 Senate seats · DC gets 3 electors (23rd Amendment)
Winner-take-all: 48 states award ALL electors to the popular vote winner in that state.
Exceptions: Maine and Nebraska use proportional/congressional district method.

If no one reaches 270: House chooses president (each state delegation gets 1 vote), Senate chooses VP — called a contingent election.

Faithless electors: electors who don't vote for their pledged candidate — rare and most states now have laws against it.
"538 ÷ 2 + 1 = 270" — majority of the total.
Presidential Requirements & Term
Must be 35 years old · Must be a natural-born citizen · Must have been a 14-year U.S. resident · Term = 4 years · Max = 2 terms (22nd Amendment)
Presidential succession order: VP → Speaker of the House → President Pro Tempore of the Senate → Secretary of State → ...

A president can serve a max of 10 years total — 2 full terms plus up to 2 years finishing a predecessor's term.
Supreme Court & Federal Judiciary
Supreme Court Numbers
9 total justices · 1 Chief Justice + 8 Associate Justices · 5 votes needed for a majority ruling · 4 votes needed to grant certiorari (agree to hear a case) — the "Rule of Four"
Justices serve lifetime tenure ("during good behavior") — per Federalist 78.

The Court receives ~7,000–8,000 cert petitions per year but only hears about 60–80 cases.

Unanimous decisions (9-0) carry the most weight. 5-4 decisions are the most controversial and most likely to be revisited.

No set number of justices required by the Constitution — Congress sets it by law (has ranged from 5 to 10 historically).
"Rule of Four to hear, Five to decide"
Federal Court Levels
3 levels: District Courts (94 total) → Courts of Appeals/Circuit Courts (13 total) → Supreme Court (1)

All federal judges appointed by president, confirmed by Senate simple majority (51 votes), serve lifetime tenure.

Key vocabulary, models, and concepts tested on the AP U.S. Gov exam — organized by topic.

Democracy Models & Ideals
Types of Democracy
Participatory Democracy
A model of democracy that emphasizes broad, active citizen participation in politics — more than just voting. Grassroots organizing, referendums, and town halls reflect this ideal.
Compare to: Pluralist democracy (groups compete) and Elite democracy (educated few govern). The AP exam loves asking you to match a scenario to the right model.
Types of Democracy
Pluralist Democracy
Policy is made through competition among organized interest groups. No single group dominates permanently. Directly tied to Federalist No. 10 — Madison's vision of faction management.
Key idea: Government acts as an arena, not the decision-maker. Interest groups push and pull until policy reflects a balance of competing interests.
Types of Democracy
Elite Democracy
A model arguing that a small, educated, and talented minority should govern on behalf of the majority. Founders like Hamilton leaned toward this — reflected in the Electoral College and appointed Senate (pre-17th Amendment).
Core Democratic Tenet
Popular Sovereignty
Government authority comes from the people. Found in the Declaration of Independence ("consent of the governed") and the Constitution's Preamble ("We the People").
Core Democratic Tenet
Social Contract Theory
People surrender some freedoms to government in exchange for protection of natural rights. Rooted in John Locke. Central to the Declaration of Independence — if government breaks the contract, people may overthrow it.
Core Democratic Tenet
Natural Rights
Rights that belong to all people by nature, not granted by government. Locke: life, liberty, property. Jefferson adapted this to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness in the Declaration.
Core Democratic Tenet
Republicanism
Citizens elect representatives to govern on their behalf — as opposed to direct democracy. The U.S. system is a republic. Connected to Federalist 10's argument that elected filters refine public opinion.
Congressional & Representation Models
Models of Representation
Delegate Model
A legislator acts as a direct agent of constituents — votes the way their district wants, even if they personally disagree.
Trustee Model: The legislator uses their own best judgment, regardless of constituent opinion (Edmund Burke). Elected to think, not just follow.

Politico Model: Hybrid — switches between delegate and trustee depending on the issue and political context. Most realistic description of Congress.
D = Does what district says · T = Thinks for themselves · P = Picks and chooses
Models of Representation
Trustee Model
A legislator acts on their own independent judgment of the public good — elected for their wisdom, not just to mirror opinion. Associated with Edmund Burke.
Models of Representation
Politico Model
Legislators switch between delegate and trustee roles depending on the political salience of an issue. Most accurate description of how actual Congress members behave.
Elections
Gerrymandering
Manipulating the drawing of electoral district boundaries to favor a political party or group. Two key techniques: cracking (splitting opposition voters across districts) and packing (concentrating opposition voters in one district).
Baker v. Carr (1962): Courts can rule on redistricting. Shaw v. Reno (1993): Racial gerrymandering violates Equal Protection. Partisan gerrymandering is harder to challenge — federal courts have largely stepped back from it.
Legislative Process
Filibuster & Cloture
Filibuster: A Senate tactic of prolonged speech or delay to block a vote. Cloture: The mechanism to end a filibuster — requires 60 votes (3/5 of the Senate). The House has no filibuster.
Legislative Process
Divided Government
When the president is from one party and at least one chamber of Congress is controlled by the other. Often leads to gridlock — difficulty passing major legislation.
Legislative Process
Logrolling
When legislators trade votes — "I'll vote for your bill if you vote for mine." Common way to build coalitions in Congress, especially for spending bills and earmarks.
Legislative Process
Pork Barrel Spending / Earmarks
Federal funding secured for a specific district or state, often benefiting a legislator's own constituents. Also called earmarks. Used to win votes for broader legislation.
Executive Branch & Bureaucracy
Presidential Powers
Executive Orders
Directives issued by the president that carry the force of law without congressional approval. An informal/unilateral presidential power. Can be overturned by Congress or the courts.
Presidential Powers
Signing Statements
Written statements issued by the president when signing legislation, explaining how they interpret — or plan to selectively enforce — parts of a law. An informal expansion of presidential power.
Presidential Powers
Veto & Pocket Veto
Veto: President rejects a bill — Congress can override with 2/3 of both chambers. Pocket veto: If Congress adjourns within 10 days and president doesn't sign, the bill dies without a veto override option.
Bureaucracy
Iron Triangle
A stable, mutually beneficial 3-way relationship between a congressional committee, a federal agency, and a relevant interest group. Each benefits the others in policymaking.
Example: The House Agriculture Committee + the USDA + the American Farm Bureau. They cooperate to shape farm policy for mutual benefit.

Issue Networks = a looser, more fluid version of the iron triangle — includes media, think tanks, academics, more actors.
Bureaucracy
Issue Networks
A more fluid and open policy community than the iron triangle — includes interest groups, academics, journalists, think tanks, and bureaucrats who all influence policy in a given area. Less stable than iron triangles.
Bureaucracy
Discretionary & Rulemaking Authority
Discretionary authority: Agencies decide how to implement broadly written laws. Rulemaking authority: Agencies create specific regulations that carry the force of law. Both represent ways the bureaucracy makes policy.
Judicial Branch
Judicial Power
Judicial Review
The power of courts to review laws and executive actions and declare them unconstitutional. Established in Marbury v. Madison (1803). Not explicitly in the Constitution — inferred by Chief Justice Marshall.
Judicial Philosophy
Judicial Activism vs. Judicial Restraint
Judicial Activism: Courts willingly strike down laws and use constitutional interpretation to address social issues (e.g., Brown v. Board). Judicial Restraint: Courts defer to elected branches and interpret the Constitution narrowly, only striking laws clearly unconstitutional.
Judicial Philosophy
Originalism vs. Living Constitution
Originalism: Interpret the Constitution according to its original meaning at ratification (Scalia). Living Constitution: The Constitution's meaning evolves with society (Brennan, Ginsburg). Major fault line in confirmation hearings.
Judicial Process
Stare Decisis
Latin for "let the decision stand." Courts generally follow prior rulings (precedents) for consistency and stability. The Supreme Court can overturn precedent (e.g., Dobbs overturning Roe), but this is rare and significant.
Judicial Process
Amicus Curiae Brief
A "friend of the court" brief filed by a non-party (often interest groups or governments) to offer information or perspective in a Supreme Court case. One way interest groups influence judicial outcomes.
Judicial Process
Writ of Certiorari
An order from the Supreme Court to a lower court to send up the case record for review. The Court grants "cert" using the Rule of Four (4 of 9 justices must agree). The vast majority of petitions are denied.
Federalism
Federalism
Enumerated (Expressed) Powers
Powers explicitly listed in the Constitution for Congress (Article I, Section 8): declare war, coin money, regulate commerce, tax, etc. The foundation of federal authority.
Federalism
Implied Powers & the Elastic Clause
Powers not explicitly listed but inferred from the Necessary and Proper Clause (Article I, §8). McCulloch v. Maryland confirmed Congress can use implied powers to carry out its expressed powers. Called "elastic" because it stretches federal authority.
Federalism
Reserved Powers (10th Amendment)
Powers not granted to the federal government or prohibited to states are reserved to the states or the people. Foundation of state authority. Key in U.S. v. Lopez — Congress exceeded its Commerce power into state territory.
Federalism
Concurrent Powers
Powers shared by both the federal and state governments: taxing, borrowing money, establishing courts, building roads. Neither level has exclusive control over these.
Federalism
Categorical vs. Block Grants
Categorical grants: Federal money given for a specific purpose with strict conditions (e.g., school lunch programs). Block grants: Lump sum given to states with few conditions — states decide how to use it. Republicans generally prefer block grants; Democrats favor categorical.
Federalism
Unfunded Mandates
Federal laws that require states to perform certain actions without providing funding. Controversial — states must bear the cost of complying with federal policy. The Unfunded Mandates Reform Act (1995) tried to limit them.
Federalism
Dual vs. Cooperative Federalism
Dual (Layer Cake): Federal and state governments operate in separate, distinct spheres — pre-New Deal. Cooperative (Marble Cake): Federal and state governments share functions and collaborate — modern era. New Federalism: Reagan-era effort to return power to states via block grants.
Voting Models & Political Participation
Models of Voting Behavior
Rational Choice Voting
Voters make decisions by calculating their self-interest — voting for the candidate whose policies will benefit them most. Assumes voters act as rational, informed utility-maximizers.
Retrospective Voting: Voters reward or punish incumbents based on past performance — especially the economy.

Prospective Voting: Voters choose based on candidates' future policy promises, not past record.

Party-Line Voting: Voters consistently vote for the same political party regardless of candidates or issues — driven by party identification.
RRPP: Rational, Retrospective, Prospective, Party-line
Models of Voting Behavior
Retrospective Voting
Voters judge incumbents on past performance. If the economy is good, re-elect. If not, vote them out. Reagan's "Are you better off than you were four years ago?" is the classic example.
Models of Voting Behavior
Prospective Voting
Voters choose the candidate whose future policy promises they most prefer. Focuses on platforms, campaign promises, and policy positions rather than track record.
Models of Voting Behavior
Party-Line Voting
Voters consistently support candidates from one party regardless of the individual or specific issues. Party identification is the single best predictor of vote choice in the United States.
Political Participation
Factors Affecting Voter Turnout
Key factors that increase turnout: higher education, higher income, older age, competitive elections, easy registration laws, high political efficacy. Presidential elections draw more voters than midterm or local elections.
Political Participation
Political Efficacy
Internal efficacy: Belief that you understand politics and your participation matters. External efficacy: Belief that the government is actually responsive to citizen demands. Both predict likelihood of political participation.
Political Participation
Linkage Institutions
Structures that connect citizens to the government: political parties, elections, interest groups, and the media. They translate public preferences into policy demands. A major Unit 5 concept.
Political Parties & Interest Groups
Party System
Why Two Parties? (Duverger's Law)
The U.S. single-member district, winner-take-all (plurality) election system naturally produces two dominant parties. Third parties face impossible structural barriers: ballot access laws, media exclusion, and the "wasted vote" problem.
Party Change
Party Realignment & Dealignment
Realignment: A major, durable shift in the partisan loyalties of large groups (e.g., the Solid South shifting from Democrat to Republican from the 1960s–80s). Dealignment: Voters abandon both parties, rising independents — no new stable alignment emerges.
Interest Groups
Interest Groups & Lobbying
Interest groups organize around shared goals to influence government. Lobbying = direct contact with legislators or their staff. PACs donate to campaigns; Super PACs (post-Citizens United) make unlimited independent expenditures.
Interest Groups
Free Rider Problem
When a public good or policy benefit applies to everyone, individuals have an incentive to let others do the work (lobbying, activism) and "free ride." This discourages collective action and makes organizing difficult.
Public Opinion & the Media
Ideology Formation
Political Socialization
The process by which people develop their political beliefs and values. Key agents: family (most influential), school, peers, media, religion, and major political events (wars, recessions, crises).
Measuring Public Opinion
Scientific Polling — Key Concepts
Random sampling: Every person has an equal chance of being selected — key to a valid poll. Sampling error / margin of error: The range within which the true answer lies (±%). Question wording can skew results. Larger samples reduce error.
Media & Politics
Agenda Setting, Framing & Priming
Agenda setting: Media decides what issues the public thinks about. Framing: Media influences how issues are understood (crime as individual vs. systemic). Priming: Media coverage shapes which issues voters use to evaluate politicians.
Media & Politics
Traditional vs. New/Partisan Media
Traditional media: Broadcast TV, major newspapers — aimed at broad audiences, professional journalism norms. New/partisan media: Cable news, social media, podcasts — often ideologically sorted. Has contributed to political polarization and information bubbles.
Economic Policy & Ideology
Economic Policy
Fiscal Policy
Government use of taxing and spending to influence the economy. Keynesian (liberal): Increase spending in recessions to stimulate demand. Supply-side (conservative): Cut taxes to incentivize investment and growth ("trickle-down").
Economic Policy
Monetary Policy & the Federal Reserve
Control of the money supply and interest rates by the Federal Reserve (the Fed). Raising interest rates slows inflation; lowering them stimulates growth. The Fed is independent of Congress and the president.
Political Ideology
Liberal vs. Conservative — Policy Positions
Liberal/Democrat: Favors government intervention in the economy (regulation, social programs), more expansive civil rights, Keynesian stimulus. Conservative/Republican: Favors free markets, lower taxes, deregulation, supply-side economics, stricter social policy.
Additional Must-Know Concepts
Constitutional Principles
Checks & Balances
Each branch has specific powers to limit the others: Congress can override vetoes and impeach; president can veto and appoint justices; courts can declare laws unconstitutional. Prevents any one branch from becoming dominant. The entire mechanism of Federalist 51.
Civil Liberties
Selective Incorporation
The process by which the Supreme Court applies most Bill of Rights protections to state governments through the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause — case by case, right by right. Not all rights are incorporated (3rd, 7th Amendment largely not). Key cases: Gideon (6th), McDonald (2nd), Engel (1st).
Civil Liberties
Prior Restraint
Government censorship of material before it is published. Presumed unconstitutional under the First Amendment. The government faces a heavy presumption against prior restraint — established by NYT v. United States (1971).
Civil Rights
Majority-Minority Districts
Districts drawn so that racial or ethnic minorities form a majority of voters — intended to increase minority representation in Congress. Authorized under the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Challenged in Shaw v. Reno when race is the only factor.
Civil Rights
Affirmative Action
Policies that take race (or other characteristics) into account to benefit underrepresented groups. Highly contested — the Supreme Court has allowed some uses (diversity in education) while striking others (rigid quotas). Recent decisions have further restricted its use.
Elections & Campaign Finance
Campaign Finance Timeline
FECA (1971): First major campaign finance law — required disclosure and set limits. BCRA/McCain-Feingold (2002): Banned soft money to parties, restricted issue ads. Citizens United (2010): Struck down limits on independent spending — opened door to Super PACs.
Hard money: Direct contributions to candidates — regulated and capped by the FEC. Soft money: Money donated to parties for "party-building" purposes — previously unregulated; BCRA banned it for parties but Citizens United opened outside spending.
Elections & Campaign Finance
PACs vs. Super PACs
PACs (Political Action Committees): Can donate directly to candidates — capped at $5,000/candidate/election. Super PACs (post-Citizens United): Can raise and spend unlimited amounts on independent expenditures (ads, mailers) but CANNOT coordinate directly with campaigns.
Elections
Primary Elections & Caucuses
Primary: Voters choose a party's nominee via direct voting. Caucus: Party members meet in person to deliberate and choose delegates (e.g., Iowa). Open primary: Any voter may participate. Closed primary: Only registered party members may vote.
Elections
Incumbency Advantage
Sitting members of Congress have major structural advantages in reelection: name recognition, casework for constituents, franking privilege (free mail), fundraising networks, and the ability to take credit for federal spending. House incumbents win reelection at extremely high rates (~90%+).
